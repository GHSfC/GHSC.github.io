Why?

More Frequent and Severe Storms

Man-made global heating is driving an increase in intensity, and in some case an increase in frequency, of extreme weather events, including severe storms, hurricanes, cyclones and tornadoes:

The Gulf Coast, USA: Areas like New Orleans and Houston have faced hurricanes of unprecedented strength and rainfall, notably Hurricane Katrina and Hurricane Harvey.

The Caribbean: Islands like Puerto Rico, the Bahamas, and Dominica have faced devastating hurricanes in recent years (e.g., Hurricanes Maria, Dorian, and Irma), with increased intensity and destructive power.

The Philippines: This nation is regularly hit by powerful typhoons, with super Typhoon Haiyan (Yolanda) in 2013 being one of the strongest tropical cyclones ever recorded.

Florida, USA: This peninsula state, especially its eastern coast, has witnessed an increasing number of powerful hurricanes making landfall.

Bay of Bengal, South Asia: Both India and Bangladesh have faced severe cyclonic storms with increasing intensity, causing extensive flooding and damage.

Mozambique, Southeast Africa: Coastal cities like Beira have been hit hard by cyclones (e.g., Cyclone Idai and Kenneth in 2019), resulting in catastrophic flooding and humanitarian crises.

Southeast USA: Areas like the Carolinas have faced extreme rainfall events and hurricanes, with Hurricane Florence in 2018 being a notable example.

The North Atlantic: The entire Atlantic hurricane basin has seen an uptick in the number and intensity of storms, impacting a broader region, including sometimes reaching as far north as Ireland and the UK.

Pacific Islands: Nations like Fiji, Vanuatu, and Tonga are on the frontline of climate change, experiencing more intense tropical cyclones and storm surges.

Eastern Mediterranean and the Middle East: Though not traditionally associated with storms, this region has recently experienced unusual and extreme rainfall events and flooding, such as in Turkey, Greece, and Iran.
