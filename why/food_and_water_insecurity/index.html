Food and Water Insecurity

Man-made climate change has accelerated water and food insecurity across various parts of the world:

Horn of Africa (Ethiopia, Somalia, Kenya): Changing rain patterns and recurring droughts have intensified food and water scarcities, resulting in periodic famines.

United States: States like California have faced prolonged droughts affecting agriculture and water reserves. The Southwest, in particular, is vulnerable to both water scarcity and heatwaves.

Central America’s Dry Corridor (Honduras, Guatemala, El Salvador): Reduced and irregular rainfall patterns are impacting agriculture, leading to food shortages and forced migrations.

South Asia (Parts of India, Pakistan, Bangladesh): Melting Himalayan glaciers, changing monsoon patterns, and increasing temperatures contribute to both flooding and droughts, affecting agriculture and freshwater availability.

Murray-Darling Basin, Australia: This critical agricultural zone has faced reduced rainfall and rising temperatures, leading to increased water scarcity and crop failures.

Spain: Reduced rainfall and increasing temperatures have led to droughts and water scarcity in various regions, impacting agriculture and water supplies for urban areas.

Italy: Southern regions and islands like Sicily face water scarcity during hotter months, impacting agriculture, especially olives and grapes.

Andean Region (Bolivia, Peru, Ecuador): Melting glaciers are affecting freshwater sources, which communities and agriculture in the region heavily rely on.

Central Asia (Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan): The drying up of the Aral Sea and changing precipitation patterns have significantly impacted agriculture and local water supplies.

South Africa: Regions like the Western Cape, especially Cape Town, faced a near “Day Zero” scenario due to a prolonged drought, underscoring the risks of water scarcity.

Middle East (Syria, Jordan, Iraq): Over-extraction of water resources, coupled with changing rain patterns and increasing temperatures, has led to declining water tables and reduced agricultural outputs.

Germany: Uncharacteristic heatwaves and reduced rainfall in recent years have led to low water levels in rivers, affecting agriculture and even transportation.

United Kingdom: While often considered a wet country, certain areas, especially in the south-east, have faced water scarcity issues during prolonged dry spells, and there are concerns about future water supply with changing rainfall patterns.
