Escalating Sea Levels

Escalating sea levels, driven primarily by the melting of polar ice caps and glaciers and the thermal expansion of seawater as it warms, is a clear manifestation of man-made climate change:

Miami, USA: Situated in Florida’s southeastern tip, Miami experiences regular “sunny day flooding” due to its low elevation, porous limestone foundation, and the encroaching sea.

Bangladesh: As a low-lying deltaic nation, Bangladesh is exceptionally vulnerable. Millions live in areas that are prone to flooding from sea-level rise and storm surges.

Venice, Italy: Historically susceptible to flooding, Venice has witnessed more frequent and severe “aqua alta” (high water) events due to rising sea levels.

Maldives: Comprising over 1,000 coral islands, the Maldives is the lowest-lying country in the world. Even slight sea-level rise poses existential threats to these islands.

Delta of the Mekong River, Vietnam: Vital for rice cultivation, this delta is increasingly inundated, threatening agriculture and displacing communities.

Louisiana, USA (Mississippi River Delta): Beyond natural subsidence, rising sea levels are causing the loss of vital coastal wetlands and increasing the vulnerability of cities like New Orleans.

Rotterdam, Netherlands: While the Dutch have a long history of water management and have constructed an elaborate system of dykes, rising sea levels continually challenge their engineering marvels.

Sundarbans, India and Bangladesh: Home to the world’s largest mangrove forest and the Bengal tiger, the Sundarbans are facing increased saltwater intrusion and land loss.
