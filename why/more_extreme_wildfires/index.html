Why?

More Extreme Wildfires

Driven by man-made climate change, several intertwined factors are increasing the likelihood of severe wildfires. Rising temperatures lead to more prolonged dry spells, causing vegetation to become tinder-dry and more susceptible to ignition. The increasing frequency and severity of heatwaves further exacerbate this dryness, while earlier snowmelt in many regions results in longer fire seasons. Simultaneously, altered precipitation patterns, characterized by more sporadic and intense rainfall followed by prolonged dry periods, contribute to the rapid growth of underbrush, which can serve as fuel for fires. Fire seaons are now starting earlier and ending later, meaning there’s less time for forest management, to reduce the likelihood of severe wildfires. There’s also the increase of invasive species, undermining the health of forests. Together, these climate-induced factors create a more volatile environment, primed for intense and large-scale wildfires.

California, USA: The state has been grappling with increasingly severe and frequent wildfires, with notable fires such as the Camp Fire (2018) and the Mendocino Complex Fire (2018) causing immense damage.

Australia: Especially the southeastern regions like New South Wales and Victoria, which were heavily affected during the 2019-2020 bushfire season, known as “Black Summer.”

Amazon Rainforest, Brazil: The Amazon has seen a rise in wildfires in recent years, often associated with deforestation and agricultural practices.

Siberia, Russia: Expansive areas of the Siberian taiga have been affected by unprecedented wildfires, exacerbated by rising temperatures.

Greece: The country has experienced recurrent wildfires, with the summers of 2018 and 2021 seeing particularly intense fires.

British Columbia, Canada: The province has faced consecutive years of intense wildfire seasons, affecting towns, forests, and air quality.

Portugal: Wildfires, especially during the summer months, have become increasingly common, with the fires of 2017 being especially devastating.

Spain: Regions, particularly in the north, have been hit by wildfires, similarly to its neighbor, Portugal.

Chile: Central and southern parts of the country have witnessed large wildfires in the past decade, notably during 2016-2017.
